I am a machine learning researcher at [RISE Research Institutes of Sweden](https://www.ri.se/en/person/maria-bankestad), where I work at the intersection of machine learning and the physical sciences. I hold a PhD from Uppsala University, supervised by [Thomas B. Schön](https://user.it.uu.se/~thosc112/index.html). My thesis, [*Structured models for scientific machine learning: from graphs to kernels*](http://urn.kb.se/resolve?urn=urn:nbn:se:uu:diva-546022), studies how scientific structure can be built into machine learning models.

Scientific problems come with structure: the geometry of a molecule, the symmetries of a fluid flow, the physics behind a measurement. My research develops machine learning methods that take this structure seriously instead of forcing the model to learn it from scratch. The right tool depends on the problem, so I move between equivariant graph neural networks, differentiable physics, neural surrogates, and probabilistic models such as Gaussian processes.

This work falls into three connected themes: geometry and symmetry, probabilistic models and experimental design, and differentiable physics. The applications are broad, from molecular property and spectra prediction to fluid and wind-farm simulation, protein design, and structure elucidation from scattering data for drug-delivery nanoparticles. Much of it is collaborative, spanning chemistry, materials, energy, and the life sciences. You can browse it by theme and application area on my [research page]({{ '/projects/' | relative_url }}).
